# Ethics, Policy, and the Lines We Don't Cross

YouTube marketing sits in a reputational grey area the same way link building and scraping do. The technical question — "can we juice this number?" — is almost always yes. The right question is "should we, and what does it cost us if YouTube notices?" This page is the boundary discipline for the whole skill: YouTube's own published policies, how enforcement actually happens, the disclosure obligations, and the explicit list of things this skill will not do or recommend.

This is not legal advice and it is not a YouTube insider's certainty. It is a risk posture built on YouTube's own Help-Center policy pages and on being honest about what is and isn't a real ranking signal. Everything in `channel-strategy.md`, `youtube-seo.md`, `asset-templates.md` and `external-engagement.md` is governed by the limits here.

The core principle, stated once: **we build only *earned* engagement — real views, subscribers, likes, and comments won with genuinely valuable content we'd be comfortable showing the viewer, a YouTube reviewer, and the destination brand simultaneously.** If a number only looks good because one of those three doesn't know how it was produced, we don't produce it.

## The authority: YouTube's own policy pages

Four YouTube Help-Center pages are authoritative and overrule every "grow fast on YouTube" listicle. When a marketing blog and YouTube disagree, YouTube wins — and the source blogs that seed this skill are *leads to verify*, never authorities.

- **Fake-engagement policy** · `support.google.com/youtube/answer/3399767`
- **Paid product placements, sponsorships & endorsements (disclosure)** · `support.google.com/youtube/answer/154235`
- **Spam, deceptive practices & scams policy** · `support.google.com/youtube/answer/2801973`
- **Search & discovery / performance FAQ (ranking, retention, tags, thumbnails)** · `support.google.com/youtube/answer/141805`

### Fake engagement

YouTube prohibits **anything that artificially inflates views, likes, comments, subscribers, or other metrics** — whether via automated systems (bots) or by serving videos to unsuspecting viewers. Specifically named as violations: **buying or botting** views/subs/likes/comments; **linking to or promoting third-party services** that inflate metrics; promoting view-count/subscriber "gaming" sites; **sub4sub** (sub-for-sub); and testimonial/"how I bought views" content. You *are* allowed to **encourage** viewers to subscribe, like, share, or comment — engagement is legitimate when a human's genuine intent is to interact, illegitimate when it stems from coercion/deception or exists solely for financial gain. **Hiring a promoter does not shield you**: a promoter's policy-violating methods can get *your* channel removed.

### Spam, deceptive practices & scams

Prohibits content/metadata/behavior designed to mislead viewers or exploit the community. The categories that bite a traffic-grabber directly:
- **Engagement manipulation** — bots, coercion, rewards-for-engagement, sub4sub.
- **Comment spam** — high-volume / repetitive / deceptive comments to drive traffic (e.g. identical "check out my channel/site" across many videos). *This kills the naive "drop your link in other people's comments" tactic.*
- **Off-platform diversion** — thin content that exists *solely* to push viewers to an external link is spam. (A legitimate CTA to your own valuable asset is fine; a doorway video is not.)
- **Malicious clickbait** — titles/thumbnails/descriptions that don't deliver what they promise.
- **Automated/synthetic mass-production & scraped content** — high volumes of near-identical AI-generated videos (same template, AI-read scripts, repetitive AI imagery), or re-posting others' material without transformative value. *Faceless is fine; faceless content farms are not.*
- **Scams** — get-rich-quick, fake offers, phishing/fake support.

### Paid-promotion disclosure — the toggle

If a video contains a **paid product placement, sponsorship, endorsement, or other commercial relationship**, the creator **must tell YouTube** by checking the **paid-promotion box** (Studio → Content → the video → "Show more" → "My video contains paid promotion like a product placement, sponsorship, or endorsement" → Save). When checked, YouTube auto-displays an **"Includes paid promotion"** disclosure to viewers for ~10 seconds at the start. Creators/brands are **separately responsible** for legal disclosure (FTC in the US, ASA in the UK, DGCCRF in France) — checking the box satisfies YouTube, not necessarily the law. **Relevant even for promoting one's own commercial assets:** under the endorsement definition, if the creator/brand relationship isn't obvious, lean toward disclosure.

### Search, discovery & metadata

Ranking is **per-video performance + relevance**, with real viewers — there is no "seed it and the algorithm amplifies" mechanic; artificial views are stripped, not counted. Titles & thumbnails must be **accurate** and follow the thumbnail policy; deceiving/clickbaity, shocking, indecent, or LOUD (ALL CAPS / `!!!!!`) packaging turns viewers away **and can trigger removal**. And the honest truths the blogs get wrong (see "Myths" below) all anchor here.

## How enforcement actually happens

Two mechanisms, both real, both worth fearing for a channel you're investing in.

### Metrics stripped / not counted

Artificial traffic is **not counted**; bot or purchased subscribers get **stripped**. So the immediate result of fake engagement is that you paid for nothing — the numbers evaporate — *before* any strike. Worse, the fake data poisons your own analytics so you can't tell what actually works.

### Strikes → termination

Violations get **removal + an email**. The usual path: a first offense is typically a **warning** (expires after 90 days via policy training); a repeat within 90 days → a **strike**; **3 strikes in 90 days → channel termination**. A single severe case, or a channel **dedicated** to violating (e.g. existing to run fake engagement or spam), can be **terminated immediately**. Violative URLs in the video or its metadata can get the whole video removed; spam/deception can also **suspend monetization**.

The asymmetry is the whole argument: faking engagement is fast and cheap; a terminated channel is total, sudden, and often unrecoverable — every video, subscriber, and the channel's entire discovery history, gone at once. That asymmetry is why we never touch the fake-engagement tactics, on any channel.

## Disclosure — the toggle *and* the law

Two independent obligations, and we satisfy both:

- **To YouTube:** check the **paid-promotion box** on any video with a paid placement, sponsorship, endorsement, or other commercial relationship. This triggers YouTube's on-screen "Includes paid promotion" notice.
- **To the viewer / regulator:** an **FTC-style clear-and-conspicuous disclosure** — a spoken line and/or visible on-screen text and a description line ("This video is sponsored by…" / "Cette vidéo contient une promotion payée"). The EU/FR equivalents (ASA, DGCCRF) point the same way.

Practical rules:
- **Promoting an owned commercial asset** (a product, a site) where the creator/brand relationship isn't obvious → disclose. When in doubt, lean transparent.
- **Affiliate / referral links** (including your own affiliate/referral parameter placed as referral) are disclosed per the platform's rules.
- **We never hide a paid relationship**, never label sponsored content as organic, never ask anyone to skip the toggle "for reach." Undisclosed-paid is the intersection of a YouTube problem and an FTC problem — we don't operate there.

## Honest truths we don't let the listicles overwrite

The source marketing guides recycle claims that the authoritative pages flatly contradict. We state the truth instead:

- **YouTube links are nofollow — traffic & brand, not SEO equity.** Outbound links (description, comments, channel/About) pass referral traffic and brand exposure, **not** PageRank. YouTube is a traffic/audience channel, not a backlink channel. The only SEO-adjacent lift is *second-surface discoverability* (the video itself ranking in Google/YouTube search) plus downstream brand searches — never the link. Link-building is a separate discipline.
- **"Buy/seed initial views to trigger the algorithm" is false and dangerous.** Ranking is per-video performance with real viewers; artificial views are stripped. *Legit* cross-promotion to earn **real** early views is fine; **paying for views is a ToS violation.**
- **Tags barely matter.** YouTube: "How important are Tags? Not important" — mainly for spelling variants. Effort belongs in title, thumbnail, spoken content, and retention.
- **Daily/weekly upload cadence is not a ranking lever.** Growth isn't correlated with time-between-uploads; quality beats volume, and mass-produced volume is itself a spam risk. Breaks don't structurally damage a channel.
- **Best-posting-time doesn't move long-term performance** for evergreen faceless tutorials. Don't obsess over the calendar.
- **Changing title/thumbnail doesn't "ping" the algorithm** — it can re-rank a video only because *viewers behave differently* with new packaging. "Don't change what's working."

Being honest about these protects your effort and risk budget from tactics that don't do what the blogs claim.

## WHAT WE NEVER DO

Drawing the line clearly. Regardless of channel, property, or how much faster it would be, this skill does not do, recommend, generate, or assist with:

- **Buying or botting views, subscribers, likes, or comments.** Any service, bot, or app that inflates metrics. Named violation; metrics are stripped and strikes accrue.
- **Sub4sub / engagement-for-engagement schemes.** "Sub to me, I sub to you," like-for-like, comment pods, or any rewards-for-engagement arrangement.
- **View-bots, click-farms, or any artificial-traffic source** — to a video, a Short, or the destination site (fake clicks/installs poison data and are fraud-adjacent).
- **Promoting or linking to third-party metric-inflation services** (view/sub "gaming" sites). Named explicitly in the fake-engagement policy.
- **Comment-link spam on others' videos.** No high-volume, repetitive, or templated "check out my channel/site" comments. External comments are **value-first and disclosed**, most often with no link at all (see `external-engagement.md`).
- **Misleading thumbnails or titles (deceptive clickbait).** No title/thumbnail that promises what the video doesn't deliver, no ALL CAPS / `!!!!!`, nothing shocking or indecent for the click. Accurate packaging only.
- **Doorway / off-platform-diversion videos.** No thin content whose only purpose is to funnel viewers to a link. The video must earn the watch on its own.
- **Mass-produced / inauthentic content.** No content-farm output: near-identical AI-templated videos at volume, AI-read scripts with no transformative value, or scraped/re-posted material. Faceless ≠ farm — every video is genuinely informative and original. Prose gets a humanization pass precisely so it reads human and isn't inauthentic slop.
- **Undisclosed paid promotion.** No skipping YouTube's paid-promotion toggle or the FTC-style disclosure on sponsored or commercial-relationship content. (Correctly disclosed + toggled paid promotion is fine — that's the only acceptable form.)
- **Asking anyone to violate policy on our behalf** — no hiring a promoter who bots/spams, no asking an editor or partner to hide a paid relationship. A promoter's violation is our channel's risk.

When a request lands in this territory, the response is to **name the boundary, cite the specific YouTube policy or the FTC obligation it crosses, explain the enforcement asymmetry, and propose the earned-engagement variant** that reaches the same goal defensibly. There is almost always a legitimate version of the objective: better packaging instead of bought clicks, genuine cross-promotion instead of seeded views, a value-first comment instead of a link drop, a disclosed sponsorship instead of a hidden one.

## When in doubt, three questions

Before publishing a video, posting a comment, or running any promotion:

1. **Did this engagement come from a real person who genuinely chose to watch/like/comment?** If a number was produced by a bot, a purchase, or a trade, it's fake — strip the tactic, not the disclosure.
2. **Would this survive a YouTube reviewer and the viewer both seeing exactly how it was made?** If you'd want to hide the method during a review, that's the signal.
3. **Would I do this to a channel I couldn't afford to lose?** Termination is all-or-nothing. If a tactic is too risky for the channel you care about, it's too risky, full stop.

These aren't tests of whether you'd get caught. They're tests of legitimacy — and legitimate YouTube marketing survives scrutiny from the platform, the viewer, and your own brand standards. Same boundary discipline that applies to scraping and link building: the capability is rarely the question; the legitimacy is.
